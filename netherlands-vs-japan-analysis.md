# Netherlands 2-2 Japan — FIFA World Cup 2026 Group F: Tactical Analysis

**Match:** Netherlands 2-2 Japan  
**Competition:** FIFA World Cup 2026, Group F (Matchday 1)  
**Date / Venue:** June 14, 2026, AT&T Stadium, Arlington/Dallas  
**Referee:** Ismail Elfath  
**Attendance:** 69,285 [ESPN / Opta]

## 1. Result & Group Context

A cautious first half exploded into a 2-2 thriller after the break. The Netherlands led twice through Virgil van Dijk and Crysencio Summerville; Japan came back twice through Keito Nakamura and a very late Daichi Kamada header. The point each leaves **Sweden** top of Group F after their 5-1 win over Tunisia, with Netherlands and Japan both on one point [ESPN].

## 3. First Half: Dutch Territory, Japanese Discipline

The half was best described as a “feeling-out” period [Sporting News]. The Netherlands dominated the ball — **69% possession** and 4 shots to Japan’s 3 [Sofascore] — but struggled to turn that control into clear chances.

Japan’s 3-4-2-1 defended as a **compact 5-4-1/5-2-3** low-to-mid block. They conceded the flanks in deep areas, collapsed into the box, and forced the Dutch to play in front of them. Key defensive actions underlined that approach:
- Japan made **10 of 11 tackles** successfully (91%) [Sofascore].
- They finished the game with **32–33 clearances** to the Netherlands’ 22–23 [Sofascore / ESPN / Opta].

The Dutch looked to progress through the half-spaces and wide crosses. Malen forced an early save from Zion Suzuki, Gakpo fired over, and Van de Ven headed off target, while Nakamura rolled a low shot just wide before halftime [Guardian / Sporting News]. It ended 0-0, with neither side creating a “big chance” [ESPN].

---

## 4. Second Half: Four Goals, Three Tactical Shifts

### 51′ — Van Dijk opens from a set-piece (Netherlands 1-0)

The breakthrough came from a dead-ball situation. Ryan Gravenberch delivered from the right and Van Dijk rose unmarked to power a header past Suzuki [Sporting News / ESPN]. Opta valued the chance at only **0.060 xG** — a classic set-piece mismatch rather than a high-probability opening [Opta].

### 57′ — Nakamura equalizes on the break (1-1)

Japan responded within six minutes. Takefusa Kubo received a cutback on the edge of the area and slipped the ball to Nakamura, whose low drive took a deflection off Jan Paul van Hecke and beat Bart Verbruggen [Guardian / ESPN]. Opta: **0.083 xG, right-footed, outside the box** [Opta]. It showed Japan’s ability to punish Dutch transitions when the full-backs were caught high.

### 64′ — Summerville’s individual brilliance (2-1)

Crysencio Summerville received the ball on the right, **ignored Denzel Dumfries’ overlapping run**, cut inside onto his left foot, and curled a precise shot in off the far post [Guardian / ESPN]. Dumfries’ run pulled Japan’s left side away, creating the lane. Opta: **0.019 xG, left-footed goal** — the lowest-xG finish of the game [Opta].

### 88′/89′ — Kamada’s chaotic equalizer (2-2)

After Moriyasu threw on fresh attackers, Japan won a late corner. Junya Ito’s delivery was met by substitute Koki Ogawa; his header glanced off Daichi Kamada and wrong-footed Verbruggen [Guardian / ESPN]. Opta: **0.140 xG, headed goal** — the highest-xG chance of the four goals [Opta]. Japan’s late push was quantified by their **52% possession after halftime** and seven second-half shots [Sofascore].

## 6. Tactical Themes

### Netherlands: Width, Crosses, and Aerial Superiority — But No Cutting Edge

Ronald Koeman’s 4-3-3 was structurally dominant:
- **Build-up through the back three / pivot:** Van Dijk (95 accurate passes), Van Hecke (87), and De Jong (69) were the main progressors [Opta].
- **Wide overloads:** Dumfries (33 passes, 8 throw-ins, 2 SCA) and Van de Ven (41 passes, 10 throw-ins) pushed high; Gakpo and Summerville stayed wide or drove inside [Opta].
- **Crossing game:** 21 crosses, with Reijnders surprisingly contributing 7, Dumfries 3, Gakpo 2, Gravenberch 2 [Opta]. Gravenberch also supplied the two assists.
- **Aerial threat:** Van Dijk and Van Hecke both got on the end of deliveries; the Oranje won **74% of aerial duels** [Sofascore].

The problem was **chance quality**. With 10 shots inside the box and 33 touches in the opposition box, the Dutch still posted **0 big chances created** [ESPN]. Their goals came from a set-piece header and a low-xG individual curler rather than from systematically breaking Japan down.

Defensively, the full-backs’ aggression left space in transition. Both Japan goals originated when the Dutch defensive line was shifting.

### Japan: Compact Block, Transitions, and Bench Impact

Hajime Moriyasu set up to frustrate and counter:
- **Compact 5-4-1 low block:** Japan made **10 of 11 tackles** (91%) and registered **32–33 clearances** [Sofascore / Opta].
- **Transition threat:** Kubo, Maeda, and Nakamura were the outlets. Kubo’s assist for Nakamura was the clearest example of Japan turning defence into attack in one pass.
- **All four shots from outside the box were Japanese** — a direct contrast to the Netherlands’ entirely inside-box shot profile [Opta].
- **Bench impact:** Junya Ito produced **3 SCA and 6 crosses** in ~24 minutes, including the corner for the equalizer [Opta]. Ogawa scored/assisted with just 3 touches. Sugawara added a shot on target, a cross, and an SCA [Opta / Sofascore].

## 8. Managerial Decisions & Quotes

**Koeman tried to close the game and paid for it.** At 2-1, he replaced Summerville, Malen, and Reijnders with Koopmeiners, Depay, and Timber; later swapped Gravenberch and Gakpo for Aké and Brobbey. The idea was to lock the game down, but it gave Japan more initiative.

After the match Koeman defended the approach:

> “There was also a problem with pressure on the flanks… we didn’t defend well. Football is a funny game because, after Japan scored the second goal, they started defending as well. So we could have scored a third goal. So I don’t regret my choices.”
> — **Ronald Koeman** [SoccerNews]

**Moriyasu was pleased with the resilience but not satisfied:**

> “The Netherlands were very strong… the players were united as one, tenacious, they fought to the end. Of course, we’re not completely satisfied with just the one point for the draw.”
> — **Hajime Moriyasu** [SoccerNews]

Opta Analyst noted a historic Dutch failure: it was the **first time the Netherlands took the lead twice in a World Cup match but failed to win**, and only the **second time Japan trailed twice at a World Cup and avoided defeat** [SoccerNews / Opta Analyst].

---

## 9. Bottom Line

This was a clash of styles that produced a fair result. The Netherlands had the **structure, territory, and physical dominance** to win — more passes, more shots on target, more aerial duels, more possession — but they lacked a genuine cutting edge and switched off at two crucial moments. Japan had the **defensive discipline, transition quality, and bench freshness** to punish those lapses.

For Group F, the draw keeps both sides behind Sweden. The Netherlands’ next test is Sweden in Houston, while Japan faces Tunisia in Monterrey. Koeman must decide whether to keep chasing width and crosses or find a way to turn possession into higher-quality chances; Moriyasu can take confidence that his substitutes can change a game in minutes.

# Pre-Tournament Comments, Context, Stats & Stories

## 1. Pre-Tournament Quotes & Context

### Netherlands: Koeman’s finishing anxiety vs. self-confidence

Ronald Koeman went into the tournament publicly worried about one thing above all: **converting chances from open play**.

The warning signs came in the final friendlies:
- **1-0 home defeat to Algeria** (June 3, Rotterdam) — a late Anis Hadj Moussa winner.
- **2-1 win over Uzbekistan** (June 8) — both Dutch goals were **Cody Gakpo penalties**, and Guus Til was sent off.

After the Algeria loss, Koeman was blunt:

> “I absolutely hate losing; this was a match we should have won. We have to win these kinds of home matches; our ranking position demands it. Because you don’t put the game to bed within those first 25 minutes, you end up playing against yourselves. If you get four or five clear chances, you have to score.”
> — **Ronald Koeman**, Reuters, June 4 [Reuters]

After the Uzbekistan game he doubled down:

> “We created a lot of chances, but in two matches we only scored twice from a penalty. In these two matches, we should have scored at least three goals from open play. We won’t get very far at the World Cup like this.”
> — **Ronald Koeman**, MyKhel, June 9 [MyKhel]

He specifically named the players who had wasted big chances against Uzbekistan: **Summerville** (handball), **Malen** (chance in front of goal), and **Reijnders** (header). That made the Japan match a test of whether the message had sunk in.

Yet the squad remained defiant. Frenkie de Jong, speaking before the Japan game, said:

> “Obviously we are aware of the feelings and the vibe in the Netherlands… but it doesn’t matter. It’s not as if our self-confidence has been jeopardized. We are full of self-confidence.”
> — **Frenkie de Jong**, USA Today, June 13 [USA Today]

Koeman himself, in a FIFA interview before the tournament, framed the Netherlands as an outsider with a young core:

> “Maybe we are not considered the big favourite to win it, but we are a team that can surprise everybody.”
> — **Ronald Koeman**, FIFA, January 2026 [FIFA]

He also made clear his philosophy on youth:

> “We’re Dutch. We’re not afraid to involve young players – we’re used to it. If I have a 30-year-old and a 22-year-old playing at the same level, I’ll pick the 22-year-old because he’s the future.”
> — **Ronald Koeman**, FIFA [FIFA]

Virgil van Dijk backed him:

> “Koeman gives us a sense of calm. He knows how to prepare a team for big matches. We believe in Koeman and his staff.”
> — **Virgil van Dijk**, FIFA [FIFA]

### Japan: Moriyasu’s ambition, injury heartbreak and the “whiteboard”

Hajime Moriyasu arrived in North America with genuine belief. Japan had beaten **Brazil** and **England** in the previous eight months and were on a **six-match winning streak** heading into the World Cup, capped by a **1-0 win over Iceland** in Maya Yoshida’s 127th and final cap [FIFA / Sportskeeda].

Moriyasu set the bar high:

> “Even when we beat opponents we had never defeated before — like Brazil last year or England this March — the team was of course happy, but then immediately shifted focus. I sense that there is joy but also a calm and steady awareness that there is another goal beyond that.”
> — **Hajime Moriyasu**, Reuters, June 1 [Reuters]

And in a buoyant SCMP interview:

> “Japan coach Hajime Moriyasu believes his team can win the World Cup after historic friendly wins over Brazil and England.”
> — **SCMP**, June 2026 [SCMP]

But he was also realistic about Group F:

> “We have to survive this stage no matter what… The top of the top talent is found in the Netherlands team. Any team from this group could win.”
> — **Hajime Moriyasu**, The Star, June 13 [The Star]

That balance — ambition tempered by respect for the group — defined Japan’s approach.

The biggest emotional story was the loss of captain **Wataru Endo**. The Liverpool midfielder had fought back from an ankle injury, only to withdraw on the opening day of the tournament and announce his **retirement from international football**. Moriyasu called it a decision that “had to be made”:

> “We had him try the best he could and also had the medical staff looking after him. We discussed that, throughout the World Cup, it may be difficult for him to perform for the entire period of the games.”
> — **Hajime Moriyasu**, Empire of the Kop / Daily Mail [Empire of the Kop]

Japan also lost **Kaoru Mitoma** (hamstring) and **Takumi Minamino** (ACL) before the tournament [Sportskeeda]. **Ko Itakura** was named the new captain [The Star].

## 3. The Best Story: Moriyasu’s Whiteboard Timekeeping

During the Japan-Netherlands game, TV cameras caught Moriyasu and his staff holding up a **small whiteboard with numbers on it** to show the players how many minutes remained. The board counted down from three to one as Japan chased the late equalizer [The Sun / Sportskeeda].

It worked: Japan scored in the 89th minute. Afterwards, fans on social media called it “genius” and “old-school,” while ITV pundit **Ange Postecoglou** joked he wished it had said “be brave” instead. **Gary Neville** quipped that when he saw a “6” board at Valencia, it referred to goals conceded [The Sun].

For a coach whose entire tournament narrative was about survival, discipline and maximizing limited resources, the whiteboard was a perfect metaphor.

## 4. How the Pre-Tournament Themes Played Out

- **Koeman’s finishing fear proved prophetic.** The Netherlands created more territory and shots but needed a set-piece header and a low-xG curler to score. They never created a “big chance” in open play.
- **Moriyasu’s survival mindset was visible.** Japan defended deep, used the bench brilliantly, and relied on set-piece precision for the equalizer — exactly the kind of resourceful football he had preached.
- **The injury losses didn’t break Japan.** Without Endo, Mitoma and Minamino, they still took a point from their toughest group opponent.
